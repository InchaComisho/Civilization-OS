## Abstract

**Civilization OS** is a conceptual framework that describes the invisible operating system underlying human civilization.

Modern civilization faces multiple interconnected crises: climate change, resource depletion, soil degradation, water scarcity, ocean ecosystem decline, energy instability, urban overconcentration, social fragmentation, and the rapid development of artificial intelligence.

At first glance, these appear to be separate policy or technological problems.  
However, this document interprets them as symptoms of a deeper structural issue: a design failure in the underlying operating principles of civilization.

The current Civilization OS has been driven by several dominant paradigms:

* Linear thinking
* Consumption-based development
* Dualism between humanity and nature
* Anthropocentrism
* Growth-centered economics
* Externalization of nature
* Short-term profit optimization

The purpose of this document is to define the concept of Civilization OS, analyze the structural problems of the current civilization model, and provide a foundation for designing the next Civilization OS.

The detailed design of the next Civilization OS, including planetary circulation, water systems, soil, oceans, cities, AI, and compatibility with natural law, will be addressed in a separate document.

## 1. Definition: What Is Civilization OS?

Civilization OS refers to the deep layer of assumptions, values, institutions, technologies, infrastructures, and worldviews that determine how a civilization operates.

In computing, an operating system coordinates applications, hardware, memory, data, and user interaction.  
In the same way, Civilization OS coordinates the underlying logic of society.

It includes:

* Worldview
* Value systems
* Economic structures
* Social institutions
* Science and technology
* Urban infrastructure
* Food systems
* Water systems
* Energy systems
* Information systems
* Artificial intelligence
* The relationship between humanity and nature

Civilization OS exists deeper than individual policies or technologies.

Policies are shaped by institutions.  
Institutions are shaped by values.  
Values are shaped by worldview.

Therefore, to address the crisis of civilization at its root, it is not enough to improve individual technologies.  
We must examine the operating principles that determine how those technologies are used.

## 2. Problem Statement

Modern civilization possesses extremely advanced technologies.

Humanity has developed powerful systems for electricity generation, communication, transportation, medicine, finance, artificial intelligence, space development, construction, agriculture, and data analysis.

Yet the global environment continues to deteriorate.  
Resource imbalance continues to grow.  
Food and water insecurity are increasing.  
Urban systems are becoming more fragile.  
Social and ecological instability are becoming more visible.

The central question is not simply:

**Do we lack technology?**
